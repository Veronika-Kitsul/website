The Algorithm Knew Before I Did, or How TikTok Correctly Identified Me as (Masking) Autistic?

January 19, 2024

I can’t say I’m a frequent on TikTok; more so that I check it out about twice a week when I get bored. I enjoy videos on pottery, traveling, and sports, but lately… I’ve been getting a lot of videos on psychology and ‘self-help’ about mental trauma. All of them are very related to what I was going through mentally at the time and I was actively engaging with them. The algorithm never got my interests or other things about me wrong.

Slowly, my mental health videos started dissipating with more and more videos about ADHD, then about the people living with ADHD and autism both and eventually, on autism itself. A lot of autistic creators shared their experiences of living with autism and, for some reason, I started getting them too. The same thing happened to me about a year ago. I was on TikTok even more rarely at that time and at some point I just deleted TikTok because of my constant issues with phone memory and didn’t give it much thought. I do recall seeing occasional videos from autistic creators, but I did not pay any attention to them, simply scrolled past, as I considered it something irrelevant to me.

This time, though, I started wondering:

| wait, I am getting so many videos about autism lately; why?

I stopped for a moment and decided that I needed to discover more about it, because, frankly, I had no idea how to describe autism to anyone. All that popped into my memory were non-verbal children, whose physical and intellectual development was inhibited and they needed special support around the clock. But, I am not one of them, so what is going on here? I wish I remembered more details that led up to this, but I really don’t as I did not pay much attention to how my For You Page was changing over time — maybe we all should!

On December 20th, 2023, I messaged my boyfriend: “I get so many videos about autism???” and rushed to take some online tests. Ah, the online tests we all love and adore, and yes, I had no better solutions. If I would even dare to tell someone “I would like to explore whether I could have autism,” they would laugh in my face, as they did when I raised concerns about me having ADHD. Upon my mention of wanting to get evaluated for ADHD, my therapist briskly shook off such ideas, and every time that I was literally describing to her my constant search for excitement (so clearly ADHD) and new, non-boring things for entire sessions, she was like ‘hmmm, what do you think about that?’ (that I so obviously have ADHD).

Side note on ADHD here: I am super confident I have had ADHD since childhood and while I have not gotten an official diagnosis because I did not seek one, it does not change the very real fact that I’m pronouncedly ADHD. Unlike with autism, I never masked my ADHD. The only reason I was never evaluated for it is that I was doing amazing at school and my family has no knowledge of what is ADHD (or autism) whatsoever and they just suppressed my ADHD (and autistic) traits as they could.

The online tests, although being such a faulty solution, helped me see what traits are considered autistic and how they could manifest in someone. I had some overlaps, but nothing that really struck “autism, here it is!” All of my overlapping qualities seemed to be much more subtle than those described to me by all of those websites. Curious as to why TikTok’s algorithm thought such videos would be interesting to me, I started paying more attention to them and dedicated all of my procrastination time to discovering what exactly is autism. But, as it was exactly during my final exams for the semester, I decided to keep myself away from actively researching everything about it and saving it for after the exams.

It was through TikTok that I discovered a concept of masking Autism and how those in underprivileged positions, usually women, poor people, queer people, trans people, and people of color are the ones to mask most often. We camouflage having Autism at all just not to seem different from others. However, it’s not on purpose: the neurotypical (disabilities such as ADHD and Autism and others are under an umbrella-term neurodivergent as opposed to neurotypical brain structures) rules were so deeply imposed on us by our families, friends, teachers, and just society that we oftentimes have no idea about our disability. This is precisely why it’s so hard to recognize autism in adults and honestly, I don’t believe that someone else can see your autism better than you do given that you have all of the information about it. I always suppressed my autistic sensitivity to sounds and have never communicated that with anyone. Until recently, I had no idea it was an autistic trait at all. I subconsciously started wearing noise-cancelling headphones everywhere I go and leaving my room without them would be a nightmare to me.

Now, this article is not about my autism per se, but just about how I discovered I’m autistic, even though I do plan to write about autism and my experiences with it.

So, when I was done with my final exams (honestly, right after I finished my last Chinese exam), I went into the bookstore in my town, looking for some good books about autism in adults and masking autism. Spending there a good hour to find this one book turned out to be life-changing. So that you don’t have to spend so much time and if you would only read one book about autism, read this one — Unmasking Autism by Dr. Devon Price.

Through the book, I realized that what people used to call Asperger’s also falls on the autism spectrum. I somehow did not connect the two at all (well not somehow, you saw my description of autism before I knew better). When I was around 13, I read The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time by Mark Haddon (in Ukrainian, of course). The boy in the book was just such a genius; he terribly struggled socially, but he was a math genius. And the 13-year-old me was romanticizing his intellect and wished to be like him. Turns out, I am not the only one to ever think so about people with Asperger’s. When I confided to a friend that I discovered I’m autistic, she told me that her cousin has Asperger’s and that she is crazy good at math and my friend is jealous of their cousin for such intellect.

Now, I want to highlight that it’s important to understand that we have our fair share of struggles in the world, especially social struggles. However, things like being crazy good at math or having special interests also make us very good in other aspects of living.

As I was reading through Unmasking Autism, with every page I became more and more confident that this is me and I find belonging here. I kept watching videos by autistic creators and found myself in so many of them. Masking autism along with ADHD so perfectly explains my struggles and previous life experiences (but more about this part for another time).

Today, I proudly identify as Autistic. It’s been a very interesting journey and, honestly, thanks, TikTok. Ever since I found out about my true self, I became so much happier. I don’t think anymore that I’m wrong or weird for not ‘getting it’ in social settings or for caring about things that actually bother me mentally but seem like nothing to everyone else.

If you or anyone you know suspects you might have autism, I highly recommend the book and also just watch videos by autistic creators to find out more. Autism is not a ‘disease’ that needs clinical diagnosis; it just speaks to your altered brain structure that differentiates how you behave socially from many other neurotypical people. Quoting Dr. Price and their Instagram post from January 5th, 2024, “[self-identification as Autistic] is a personal and social process of realization, and nobody can tell you what’s right for you.” If you find out that you are autistic — I’m glad you were able to figure that out about yourself, it feels both scary and also very relieving, because that explains your past struggles. If you find out that you are not, we are glad to have you as an ally who knows so much about autism now.
